My Actifit Report Card: February 6 2019

Nian turns into a fairy child and a new monster Xi (夕) comes on the stage. “The story of how Nian kills Xi,” created by fairytale writer Guo Mingzhi, was first published in 1981 and was adapted into a papercut animation in 1984.
According to the animation, monster Xi harasses (骚扰，sāo rǎo) people every New Year’s Eve. The kitchen god (灶君，zào jūn) fights against it to protect people but fails, so he asks other gods for help. Fairy child Nian volunteers and uses red silk band and firecrackers to kill it.
To commemorate (纪念，jì niàn) hero Nian, people call the New Year’s Eve “Chu Xi (除夕)”, meaning kill Xi, and the first day of Chinese New Year, Nian. They also employ firecrackers and red couplets (evolving from red silk bands) to safeguard their homes.

My Actifit Report Card: February 5 2019

According to “Nian’s story” published in the Xinmin Evening News (《新民晚报》) in 1959, the monster Nian has two dreadful heads, four ears and eight legs and lives on eating people. It sleeps all the year round until the night of New Year’s Eve when it crawls out of its cave (洞穴，dòng xué) and looks for food.
Nian is afraid of (害怕，hài pà) daylight, red color and especially loud noise. As a result, people put up red couplets (对联，duì lián) on doors, red paper cuttings (剪纸，jiǎn zhǐ) on windows and light up red lanterns (灯笼，dēng lóng) on the New Year’s Eve. They also set off firecrackers (放鞭炮，fàng biān pào) and beat drums (打鼓，dǎ gǔ) to scare it away.

My Actifit Report Card: February 4 2019

猪脑子 Pig brain
Even though pigs are as smart as dogs, they still tend to be depicted as stupid, lazy, and dirty. The term “pig brain” is usually used to describe someone with a low-level intelligence or bad memory. For example:
Nǐ zhēn shi ge zhū nǎo zi a zhè dōu jì bù zhù!
你真是个猪脑子啊，这都记不住！
What an idiot! You can’t even remember this!
